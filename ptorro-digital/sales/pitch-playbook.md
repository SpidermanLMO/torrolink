# ROADBLOCK — PTorro Digital Pitch Playbook
### Sales Scripts + Objection Handling
_For Beachhead field ops — walk-in cold sales_
_Open this and the Rundown on your phone BEFORE you walk in._

---

## The One Idea Behind Every Pitch

You are not selling a website. You are selling **the next call they're currently losing.**

Every day they're invisible on Google, a customer who heard their name, Googled them, found nothing, and called a competitor. The website just stops that bleed. Frame everything as **money already walking out the door** — not a new expense.

> **Mantra:** "It's not $529 you spend. It's $529 that stops you from losing thousands."

---

## The Setup

**Before you walk in:**
- You have a mock site already built for this specific business (Snake Eyes built it)
- You have the URL pulled up on your phone or tablet — ready to show in one tap
- You know the business name, what they do, roughly what one job is worth
- You have the Stripe payment link ready to text on the spot

**Rule:** Never pitch without the site already built. The site IS the pitch. The owner is not deciding *whether* to build a site — they're deciding whether to keep the one already sitting on your screen with their name on it.

---

## The Walk-In (Opening Hook)

The opening has one job: earn 30 seconds. Lead with **what you already did for them**, not what you want from them.

> "Hey — I'm Beachhead with PTorro Digital. I'm not here to sell you anything today. I already built your business a website, and I just want to show it to you for 30 seconds. If you hate it, I walk out, no hard feelings. Fair?"

**Why it works:** "already built" = curiosity + reciprocity. "Not selling / if you hate it I walk" = drops their guard. "Fair?" = a tiny yes that starts the momentum.

**Variants by situation:**

If they have a bad website:
> "I work with local businesses on getting found online. I pulled up your current site — and honestly, you do better work than that site shows. So I rebuilt it. 30 seconds, can I show you?"

If owner isn't there:
> "Is the owner around? I built them something and I want them to see it — 2 minutes, no pitch. When's the best time to catch them?"

If they seem busy:
> "You're slammed — I'll be 20 seconds. I built your business a website. I just want you to see the homepage, then I'm gone."

## The 30-Second Demo

**Step 1 — Hand them the phone/tablet (let THEM hold it):**
> "This is your site. Your name. Your services. Your phone number — see that button? A customer taps it, it calls you. That's it."

**Step 2 — Scroll slowly. Point to, in order:**
- Their business name at the top → *"That's you, top of the page."*
- The click-to-call button → *"One tap, your phone rings."*
- The services section → *"Everything you do, laid out."*
- The lead form → *"They fill this out, you get an email the same second — even if you're under a sink."*

**Step 3 — The transition to value (loss frame, then price):**
> "Right now, when someone hears your name and Googles you, they find nothing — and they call the next guy. This is the thing that stops that. For what you charge on one job, it pays for itself the first time the phone rings."

**Step 4 — State the price, then go quiet:**
> "It's $529, one time. The site's already built — it can be live on your own name by this week."

**PAUSE. Say nothing. Let the silence do the work.** Whoever talks first loses leverage. Count to ten in your head if you have to.

## The Price Anchor (say it in THIS order)

Never lead with $529. Lead with what everyone else charges, so $529 sounds like a steal — because it is.

> "Real quick, so you know I'm not overcharging you. A US freelancer for a site like this is $1,500 to $5,000 and takes a month or two. A local Austin agency is $3,000 to $10,000 and locks you in a contract. Doing it yourself on Wix costs you 30-plus hours of your own time — that's billable time you're not on a job — and it still looks like a template."
>
> "Mine is $529. One time. Already built. Live this week. No contract — you own it."

**The cost-of-inaction line (use the number for THEIR trade — see chart below):**
> "And here's the part most people miss. Doing nothing isn't free. A guy in your trade quietly loses around [3 jobs a month] just from people who couldn't verify you online. That's [$1,500] a month walking out the door. The site costs less than one of those lost jobs — once."

## Risk Reversal — THE GUARANTEE (use this every time)

This is your strongest close. Most owners aren't saying no — they're saying "what if this is a waste of money?" Kill that fear out loud.

> **"Here's how I take all the risk off you: put down a $265 deposit today. You've got a full 72 hours to look it over, sleep on it, show your spouse — change your mind for any reason and I refund every penny, no questions. And if you go live and it's not exactly what you pictured, we keep fixing it until it is. You're not gambling $529. You're risking nothing."**

**Why this matters:** A guarantee doesn't just reduce risk — it signals confidence. A rep who guarantees the work is a rep who isn't worried about the work. Say it with a straight face and full eye contact.

**The two-part promise (memorize):**
1. **72-hour, no-questions money-back** on the deposit.
2. **"We fix it until you love it"** — revisions included before go-live.

---

## Honest Urgency (real, not fake)

Never invent fake deadlines — owners smell it. Use the urgency that's actually true:

1. **The built site is real and finite.** "I built this one for you. I only build a handful per area. If you pass, I move on and the next [plumber] in the area gets my time instead."
2. **Lost calls compound daily.** "Every week you wait is another week of those Google searches going to a competitor. The site doesn't help you the day you finally decide — it helps you the day it's live."
3. **Seasonal, where true.** (HVAC in summer, etc.) "We're heading into your busy season — this is the worst possible month to be invisible."

> "I can hold this exact site for you for 48 hours. After that I can't promise it — I've got other businesses lined up in this area."

## The 5 Objections — Word for Word

### 1. "I already have a website."

> "Perfect — can I see it real quick?"

*(Pull it up on your phone. If it's bad:)*
> "So this is what a customer sees when they Google you. Be honest — does this look like the quality of work you actually do? Mine loads in under 2 seconds on a phone, has a call button and a lead form, and it's already built. Let me show you side by side — 20 seconds."

*(If it's actually decent:)*
> "That's genuinely solid — you're ahead of most around here. We specialize in trades. If you ever want a refresh or a second site for a different service area, hang onto my card." *(Leave card. Move on. Don't waste a built site fighting a real one.)*

---

### 2. "I can't afford it."

> "I get it — money's tight, that's exactly why this matters. What do you charge for a typical [job]?"

*(Wait. Usually $200–$2,000+.)*

> "So one call off this site covers it and then some. You're not spending $529 — you're investing it to land your next handful of customers. And if work's slow right now? That's the exact moment you need to be findable, not invisible."

*(Still hesitant — bring in the guarantee + payment plan:)*
> "Tell you what — split it. $265 today, $265 when it goes live on your own name. And the $265 today is fully refundable for 72 hours. If it doesn't feel right, you get it all back. That's about as no-risk as it gets."

---

### 3. "I'll think about it."

> "Totally fair — what's the one thing you'd want to think through? Price, timing, or whether it'll actually bring calls?"

*(Listen. Whatever they name IS the real objection. Address only that.)*

> "Here's the honest situation: I built this exact site for you, today. I only do a few per area. If I walk and you decide in two weeks, odds are I've moved on and given that time to another business nearby. I can hold it 48 hours."

*(Then de-risk the decision so 'thinking' isn't necessary:)*
> "And you don't actually have to be sure today — that's what the 72-hour refund is for. Lock it with $265, take three days, change your mind for any reason and I refund it. You can decide now and still 'think about it' with zero risk."

---

### 4. "My nephew/friend does websites."

> "That's great — is it done? Or is it one of those 'coming soon' things that's been coming soon for a year?"

*(Pause. Let them admit it.)*

> "No knock on family — but here's the difference: I walked in with yours already built, it goes live this week, and if anything breaks you call us and it's handled. Favors don't come with deadlines or support. This does."

*(If the nephew's site is real and decent: leave a card, walk away clean.)*

---

### 5. "I use Facebook / Yelp / Google Maps."

> "Those are great for people who already know you. But when a stranger searches 'plumber near me' at 11pm — Google shows websites, not Facebook pages. That's the customer you're missing."

> "And here's the thing nobody tells you: you don't own that Facebook page. They can change the rules, charge you for reach, or shut it down tomorrow. Yelp takes a cut on leads. Your own website is yours forever, and every lead off it is 100% yours — no middleman."

## Closing Lines

**Assumptive close (default — act like it's happening):**
> "Let's get you live. I'll text you the link to lock it in with the $265 deposit right now — what's the best number for you?"

**Risk-reversal close (for the nervous):**
> "You've got nothing to lose here. $265 today, refundable for 72 hours, we fix it till you love it. Worst case you get your money back in three days. Best case your phone starts ringing. Want me to send the link?"

**Question close (for the quiet ones):**
> "What would have to be true for this to be a yes for you today?"

**Walk-away close (only if they're truly dragging):**
> "No pressure at all. Card's right here — when you're ready, the site's ready. But I can't promise this exact one will still be waiting. Your call."
